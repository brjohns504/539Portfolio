Better information, better education

College tuition is rising. Students and parents question the accessibility of a degree. Faculty and administrators worry about college quality. How can better institutional information capacities help each of these problems?

Learn more »

It Takes A Village

Effective information processes require involvement from everyone at an institution -- faculty, staff, administrators, and institutional research. Everyone must help to answer pivotal questions about what pieces of information best support decision-making, how those pieces of information can best be collected, and how information ought to be distributed to make decisions. Unless everyone is involved in pondering these questions, colleges will end up with a harmful, siloed approach to data.

Good Bricks Build Buildings

Extraordinarily complicated information products are the result of simple data building blocks. Colleges must approach database management with this truth in mind. If an institution fails to consistently collect and store as distinct the most basic data elements -- grades, graduation dates, demographic information -- it will have little hope of acheiving the more complicated analysis and reporting institutions so often need.

Design A Backhoe, Don't Hire More People With Shovels

Too many institutions respond to strain on information capacities by hiring more staff. But rarely do such expansions in manpower solve flawed organizational information practices that exacerbate such strain in the first place. Many institutions could save an enormous amount of scare resources if they did the hard work of being more intentional about information collection, management, and retrieval.
